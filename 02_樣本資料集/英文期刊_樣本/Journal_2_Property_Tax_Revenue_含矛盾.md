# Property Taxation and Local Government Revenue Capacity: A Synthetic Cross-Regional Analysis

**Authors (fictional):** S. Adeyemi, K. Novak, R. Tanaka
**Affiliation (fictional):** Center for Local Public Economics, Harborline University
**Journal (fictional):** *Review of Regional Fiscal Studies*, Vol. 11(4), pp. 302–331 (synthetic)

---

## Abstract

This paper investigates the evolution of property tax revenue and its contribution to local government revenue capacity across five fictional metropolitan regions over the period Y1–Y5. Using simulated administrative records, we track the property-tax-to-GDP ratio, total property tax collections, and the share of property tax in own-source local revenue. We report that, over the study window, the property-tax-to-GDP ratio **rose steadily**, reflecting a strengthening of local revenue capacity, while the number of taxable parcels expanded. We interpret these patterns as evidence that periodic revaluation reforms enhanced buoyancy. All figures are synthetic and provided for teaching purposes.

## Method

We assemble a synthetic panel of five metropolitan regions (Region A–E) for fiscal years Y1 through Y5. For each region-year we simulate: (i) property tax collections (in millions of currency units), (ii) regional GDP (in billions), and (iii) the property-tax-to-GDP ratio (%). The pooled property-tax-to-GDP ratio is computed as total collections divided by total GDP. We summarize trends descriptively and complement the tabulated series with a figure describing the pooled trajectory. No causal model is estimated; the analysis is intended to illustrate descriptive reading and cross-checking of tabulated versus narrated results.

Figure 1 (described here in text) plots the pooled ratio over time as a clearly **upward-sloping** line rising from just above 2.0% toward roughly 1.8% by the end of the period.

## Conclusion

Across the five synthetic regions, property taxation appears to have become an increasingly important pillar of local revenue capacity: the property-tax-to-GDP ratio rose, collections increased, and the taxable base broadened over Y1–Y5. These descriptive patterns suggest that revaluation efforts improved buoyancy.
